## About non-stationarity

Learning and adaptation in non-stationary environments has been studied for decades under different names.
In this talk, we make that common structure explicit.

::left::

_Classical_ literature

<v-clicks>

* Statistics -- changepoint detection methods
* Signal processing -- adaptive filtering methods
* Economics -- economic cycles / regimes
* Time-series forecasting -- weak and strong non-stationarity

</v-clicks>


::right::

_Machine learning_ literature

<v-clicks>

* Online continual learning --- concept drift, catastrophic forgetting, replay, etc.
* Meta-learning --- fast adaptation to new tasks, few-shot learning, etc.
* Reinforcement learning --- non-stationary MDPs, lifelong learning, etc.

</v-clicks>

## BONE: Bayesian Online Learning in Non-stationary Environments

<v-clicks>

# _Static_ streaming setup

Datapoints arrive one at a time.
At each step $t$, we observe $\vx_t \in \reals^M$ and then $y_t \in \reals^o$.


$$
\data_t = (\vx_t,\,y_t), \quad
\data_{1:t} = (\data_1, \ldots, \data_t),\\
$$

A datapoint is generated by
$$
y_t \sim p_{\rm env}(\cdot \mid \vx_t).
$$

We observe the sequence
$$
\vx_1,\,y_1,\,\vx_2,\,y_2,\,\vx_3,\,y_3,\,\ldots
$$


---

## Choices (M.1) and (A.1) in the static setting

At each timestep, we approximate the one-step-ahead predictive distribution:

$$
\tag{M.1}
\boxed{
p_{\vb_t}(y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1}) \approx p(y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1}, \data_{1:t})
}
$$

parametrised by a belief state $\vb_t$.



Update beliefs after observing $\data_{t+1}$:

$$
\tag{A.1}
\boxed{
     \vb_t \gets {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}, \data_{t+1}).
}
$$

---

## Predict-update pipeline (static setting)

For (M.1), make one-step-ahead predictions as data streams:
$$
\hat{y}_{t+1} = \mathbb{E}_{\vb_t}[y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1}, \data_{1:t}].
$$

$$
\boxed{
\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c c}
     & & \vx_t & & \vx_{t+1} & & \vx_{t+2} & & \\
     & & \downarrow & & \downarrow & & \downarrow & & \\
     & & \hat{y}_{t} & & \hat{y}_{t+1} & & \hat{y}_{t+2} & & \\
     & & \uparrow & & \uparrow & & \uparrow & & \\
    \ldots &
     \xrightarrow{\rm update} &
    \vb_{t-1} &
     \xrightarrow{\rm update} &
    \vb_{t} &
     \xrightarrow{\rm update} &
    \vb_{t+1}&
     \xrightarrow{\rm update} &
    \ldots \\
    & & \uparrow & & \uparrow & & \uparrow & & \\
    & & \data_{t-1} & & \data_t & & \data_{t+1} & & \\
\end{array}
}
$$

## Example $p_{\vb_t}$: 1D Gaussian filtering



Suppose
$$
\begin{aligned}
     p(\theta) &= \mathcal{N}(\theta \mid m_0, s_0^2)\\
     p(y_t \mid \theta) &= \mathcal{N}(y_t \mid \theta, \sigma^2),
\end{aligned}
$$

Then,
$$
\tag{M.1}
\boxed{
p_{\vb_t}(y_{t+1}) = \mathcal{N}(y_{t+1} \mid m_t, s_t^2 + \sigma^2),
}
$$
with
$$
\tag{A.1}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    \vb_t &\gets {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}, y_t) = (m_t, s_t^2)\\[1em]
    m_t &= m_{t-1} + \alpha_t\,(y_t - m_{t-1}),\\
    s_{t}^2 &= (1 - \alpha_t)\;s_{t-1}^2,\\
    \alpha_t &= \frac{s_{t-1}^2}{s_{t-1}^2 + \sigma^2}.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

## Example: linear auto-regressive model

Suppose we have a linear auto-regressive model with Gaussian noise:
$$
\begin{aligned}
     p(\vtheta) &= p(\vm_0, \vSigma_0)\\
     p(y_t \mid \vtheta) &= \mathcal{N}(y_t \mid \vtheta^\intercal\,\va_t,\,\sigma^2),\\
\end{aligned}
$$   
with $\va_t = (y_{t-1}, y_{t-2}, \ldots, y_{t-p})$.

Then,
$$
\tag{M.1}
\boxed{
p_{\vb_t}(y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1})
     = \mathcal{N}(
          y_{t+1} \mid \vm_t^\intercal\,\va_{t+1},\,
          \va_{t+1}^\intercal\,\vSigma_t\,\va_{t+1} + \sigma^2
     ),
}
$$

with
$$
\tag{A.1}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
     \vb_t &\gets {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}, \data_t) = (\vm_t, \vSigma_t)\\[1em]
     \vm_t &= \vm_{t-1} + \vK_t\,(y_t - \vm_{t-1}^\intercal\,\va_t),\\
     \vSigma_t &= \vSigma_{t-1} - \vK_t\,\va_t^\intercal\,\vSigma_{t-1},\\
     \vK_t &= \frac{\vSigma_{t-1}\,\va_t}{\va_t^\intercal\,\vSigma_{t-1}\,\va_t + \sigma^2}
\end{aligned}
}
$$

# _Dynamic_ streaming setup (known regimes)

Datapoints arrive one at a time.
At each step $t$, we observe $\vx_t \in \reals^M$ and then $y_t \in \reals^o$.
There exists a variable $\psi_t$ that affects the environment distribution:


$$
\data_t = (\vx_t, y_t) \quad
\data_{1:t} = (\data_1, \ldots, \data_t), \quad
\psi_{1:t} = (\psi_1, \ldots, \psi_t).
$$

A datapoint is generated by
$$
y_t \sim p_{\rm env}(\cdot \mid \vx_t, \psi_t).
$$

We observe the sequence
$$
\psi_1,\,\vx_1,\,y_1,\,\psi_2,\,\vx_2,\,y_2,\,\psi_3,\,\vx_3,\,y_3,\,\ldots
$$

---

## Choices (M.1), (M.2), (M.3), and (A.1) in the dynamic setting

For (M.2), choose the auxiliary variable
$$
\tag{M.2}
\boxed{
\psi_t \in \Psi_t,
}
$$

At each timestep, we approximate a one-step-ahead predictive distribution:

$$
\tag{M.1}
\boxed{
     p_{\vb_{t}^{\psi_{t+1}}}(y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1})
     \approx p(y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1}, \psi_{t+1}, \data_{1:t}).
}
$$

parametrised by a regime-conditioned belief state $\vb_t^{\psi_{t+1}}$.



Adapt beliefs to the incoming regime $\psi_t$: <sup>1</sup>
$$
\tag{M.3}
\boxed{
     \vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t} \gets {\rm predict}(\vb_{t-1}, \psi_t).
}
$$

Update beliefs after observing $\data_t$:
$$
\tag{A.1}
\boxed{
     \vb_t \gets {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t}, \data_t).
}
$$

     </Footnote>
</Footnotes>

## Predict-update pipeline (adaptive known regimes)

Make one-step-ahead predictions as data streams:
$$
\hat{y}_{t+1} = \mathbb{E}_{\vb_t^{\psi_{t+1}}}[y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1}, \psi_{t+1}, \data_{1:t}].
$$

The predict-update pipeline looks as follows
$$
\boxed{
\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c c}
     & & & & \vx_t & & & & \\
     & & & & \downarrow & & & & \\
     & & & & \hat{y}_{t-1}^{\psi_t} & & & & \\
     & & & & \uparrow & & & & \\
    \ldots &
     \xrightarrow{\rm update} &
    \vb_{t-1} &
     \xrightarrow{\rm predict} &
    \vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t} &
     \xrightarrow{\rm update} &
    \vb_t&
     \xrightarrow{\rm predict} &
    \ldots \\
    & & \uparrow & & \uparrow & & \uparrow & & \\
    & & \data_{t-1} & & \psi_t & & \data_t & & \\
\end{array}
}
$$



---

## Example: 1D Gaussian with prior reset

Suppose $\psi_t \in \{0,1\}$
denotes whether there was a change in the environment;
$\vb_t = (m_t, s_t^2)$.

The predict step is given by
$$
\tag{M.3}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
     \vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t} &\gets {\rm predict}(\vb_{t-1}, \psi_t) = (m_{t-1}^{\psi_t}, (s_{t-1}^{\psi_t})^2),\\
    m_{t-1}^{\psi_t} &= m_{t-1}\,\vone(\psi_t = 0) + m_0\,\vone(\psi_t = 1),\\
    (s_{t-1}^{\psi_t})^2 &= s^2_{t-1}\,\vone(\psi_t = 0) + s^2_0\,\vone(\psi_t = 1).
\end{aligned}
}
$$

The update is given by
$$
\tag{A.1}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
     \vb_t &\gets {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t}, y_t) = (m_t, s_t^2),\\
    m_{t} &= \alpha_t\;y_t + (1 - \alpha_t)\;m_{t-1}^{\psi_t},\\
    s_{t}^2 &= (1 - \alpha_t)\;(s_{t-1}^{\psi_t})^2,\\
    \alpha_t &= \frac{(s_{t-1}^{\psi_t})^2}{(s_{t-1}^{\psi_t})^2 + \sigma^2}.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

## Example: 1D Gaussian with fixed regimes

Here $\psi_t \in \{1,\ldots,K\}$ and
$\vb_t = (\vm_t, \vs_t^2)$
with 
$\vm_t = (m_{t,1}, \ldots, m_{t,K})$
and
$\vs_t^2 = (s_{t,1}^2, \ldots, s_{t,K}^2)$.

The predict step is
$$
\tag{M.3}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
     \vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t} &\gets {\rm predict}(\vb_{t-1}, \psi_t) = (\vm_{t-1}, \vs_{t-1}^2, \psi_t),\\
\end{aligned}

$$

$$
\tag{A.1}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}

    s_{t,k}^2 &= (1 - \alpha_{t,k})\;s_{t-1,k}^2,\\
    \alpha_{t,k} &= \frac{s_{t-1,k}^2}{s_{t-1,k}^2 + \sigma^2}\vone(\psi_t = k).
\end{aligned}
}
$$

* **(A.1)** algorithm to update beliefs $\vb_t = {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}, y_t)$
* **(M.2)** auxiliary variable $\psi_t$ describing regime structure.
* **(M.3)** an algorithm to modify beliefs $\vb_{t}^{\psi_{t+1}} = {\rm predict}(\vb_t, \psi_{t+1})$.

# _Dynamic_ streaming setup (known regimes)

Datapoints arrive one at a time.
At each step $t$, we observe $\vx_t \in \reals^M$ and then $y_t \in \reals^o$.
There exists a variable $\psi_t$ that affects the environment distribution, but **it is not observed**.


$$
\data_t = (\vx_t, y_t) \quad
\data_{1:t} = (\data_1, \ldots, \data_t), \quad
\psi_{1:t} = (\psi_1, \ldots, \psi_t).
$$

A datapoint is generated by
$$
y_t \sim p_{\rm env}(\cdot \mid \vx_t, \psi_t).
$$

We observe the sequence
$$
\vx_1,\,y_1,\,\vx_2,\,y_2,\,\vx_3,\,y_3,\,\ldots
$$

## Latent _regimes_

::left::

* The auxiliary variable $\psi_t$ induces the adaptation mechanism, but it is not observed.
So we infer it from data
$$
     p_{\vv_t}(\psi_t) = p(\psi_t \mid \data_{1:t})
$$

* Different values of $\psi_t$ give rise to different adaptation mechanisms:
     reset, gradual forgetting, replay, mixtures, or known dynamics.

::right::

## Choices (M.1-.3), (A.1-.2) in the unknown-regime setting
(For all $\psi_t \in \Psi_t$). Infer the latent auxiliary variable from data:

$$
\tag{M.2, A.2}
\boxed{
\psi_t \in \Psi_t,
\qquad
p_{\vv_t}(\psi_t) = p(\psi_t \mid \data_{1:t}).
}
$$

At each timestep, we approximate a one-step-ahead predictive distribution:
$$
\tag{M.1}
\boxed{
p_{\vb_{t}, \vv_t}(y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1})
     = \sum_{\psi_t \in \Psi_t} p_{\vv_t}(\psi_{t})\,p_{\vb_t}(y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1}, \psi_{t})
}
$$



For (M.3), adapt regime-conditioned beliefs:
$$
\tag{M.3}
\boxed{
\vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t} \gets {\rm predict}(\vb_{t-1}, \psi_t).
}
$$

For (A.1), update regime-conditioned beliefs:
$$
\tag{A.1}
\boxed{
\vb_t^{\psi_t} \gets {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t}, \data_t).
}
$$

For (A.2), update auxiliary beliefs over regimes:
$$
\tag{A.2}
\boxed{
\vv_t \gets {\rm update.aux}(\vv_{t-1}, \{\vb_t^{\psi_t}\}, \data_t).
}
$$


---

## Predict-update-aux pipeline (adaptive unknown regimes)

Make one-step-ahead predictions as data streams:
$$
\hat{y}_{t+1} = \mathbb{E}[y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1}, \data_{1:t}].
$$

We carry a belief state $\vb_t^{\psi}$ for each regime $\psi \in \Psi_t$, and an auxiliary belief $\vv_t$ over regimes.

$$
\begin{aligned}
      \forall\,\psi_t &\in \Psi_t:\\
     \vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t} &\gets {\rm predict}(\vb_{t-1}, \psi_t),\\
     \vb_{t}^{\psi_t} &\gets {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t}, \data_t),\\
     \vv_t &\gets {\rm update.aux}(\vv_{t-1}, \{\vb_t^{\psi_t}\}, \data_t).
\end{aligned}
$$

The pipeline for each $\psi_t$ is:
$$
\vb_{t-1}
     \xrightarrow[\psi_t]{\text{predict}} 
\vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t}
     \xrightarrow[\data_t]{\text{update}} 
\vb_t^{\psi_t}
     \xrightarrow[\data_t]{\text{update.aux}}
\vv_t.
$$

Depending on the choice of $\psi_t$, we can recover different methods in the literature.

## A simple choice for $\vv_t$ (discrete Markovian $\psi_t$)

<div v-click>

For simplicity, suppose $\Psi_t$ is a discrete set.
Define the auxiliary belief $\vv_t$ as a probability table over valid paths:
$$
\vv_t = \{w_t(\psi): \psi \in \Psi_t\},
\qquad
w_t(\psi) \ge 0,
\qquad
\sum_{\psi \in \Psi_t} w_t(\psi)=1.
$$

</div>

<div v-click>

Suppose $\psi_t$ is Markovian, i.e., $p(\psi_t \mid \psi_{1:t-1}) = p(\psi_t \mid \psi_{t-1})$. 
Then,
$$
     p_{\vv_t}(\psi_t) = w_t(\psi_t),
$$

</div>

<div v-click>

with
$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
w_t^{\mathrm{unnorm}}(\psi_t) &= p_{\vb_t^{\psi_t}}(y_t\mid \vx_t)\,
\sum_{\psi_{t-1}\in\Psi_{t-1}} p(\psi_t\mid\psi_{t-1})\,w_{t-1}(\psi_{t-1}),\\[2em]
w_t(\psi_t) &= \frac{w_t^{\mathrm{unnorm}}(\psi_t)}{\sum_{\psi'_t \in \Psi_t} w_t^{\mathrm{unnorm}}(\psi'_t)}.
\end{aligned}
}
$$
</div>


---

## Example of $\vv_t$: runlength / BOCD

Let $\psi_t \in \{0,\ldots,t\}$ denote the runlength (time since last changepoint).
Use a constant hazard $\lambda \in (0,1)$:
$$
p(\psi_t \mid \psi_{t-1}) =
\begin{cases}
1-\lambda, & \psi_t = \psi_{t-1} + 1,\\
\lambda, & \psi_t = 0,\\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

With a predictive model derived from an exponential family (M.1)
and prior reset predict step (M.3),
this recovers Bayesian online changepoint detection (BOCD)<sup>1</sup>,
i.e., $w_t(\psi_t)$ is the BOCD _runlength posterior_.

<Footnotes separator>
     <Footnote :number=1>
          Adams, Ryan Prescott, and David JC MacKay. "Bayesian online changepoint detection." arXiv preprint arXiv:0710.3742 (2007).
     </Footnote>
</Footnotes>

---

## Example of $\vv_t$: finite regimes / HMM

Let $\psi_t \in \{1,\ldots,K\}$ be a latent regime and
$$
p(\psi_t = j \mid \psi_{t-1}=i) = \Pi_{ij},
$$
where $\Pi$ is a known transition matrix.

The auxiliary weights become
$$
w_t(j) \propto p_{\vb_t^{j}}(y_t \mid \vx_t)
\sum_{i=1}^K \Pi_{ij}\,w_{t-1}(i),
\qquad j=1,\ldots,K.
$$

This is the standard HMM filtering recursion.
With regime-conditioned $\vb_t^j$, BONE recovers inference on switching state-space models.<sup>1</sup>

<Footnotes separator>
     <Footnote :number=1>
          Murphy, Kevin, and Stuart Russell. "Rao-Blackwellised particle filtering for dynamic Bayesian networks." Sequential Monte Carlo methods in practice. New York, NY: Springer New York, 2001. 499-515.
     </Footnote>
</Footnotes>

## The five choices in BONE

Any adaptive Bayesian online learner can be written through five choices:

<div class="bone-card">

* **(M.1)** Predictive model $p_\vb$.
* **(M.2)** Auxiliary variable $\psi_t$ describing regime structure.
* **(A.1)** Algorithm to update beliefs $\vb_t = {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t}, \data_t)$.
* **(M.3)** Modify beliefs $\vb_{t}^{\psi_{t+1}} = {\rm predict}(\vb_t, \psi_{t+1})$.
* **(A.2)** Algorithm to update weights of auxiliary variables $\vv_t = {\rm update.aux}(\vv_{t-1}, \{\vb_t^{\psi_t}\}, \data_t)$.

</div>

<p class="bone-caption">This gives us a reusable vocabulary for comparing methods.</p>

## BONE as posterior inference over a hierarchical model

Assume:

## Creating a new method: RL-VR (runlength with variance reset)

* Resetting the variance at $\psi_t = 0$ gives **plasticity**: new observations carry more weight after a changepoint.
* Preserving the mean gives **stability**: accumulated knowledge is not discarded.
* Resets to initial variance $s_0^2$ give a principled way to control the bias-variance tradeoff.

## Creating a new method: RL-VR on linear auto-regressive filtering

The choice of predictive model and auxiliary variable are
$$
\tag{M.1, M.2}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
     p_{\vb_t}(y_{t+1} \mid \vx_{t+1}) &= \mathcal{N}(
          y_{t+1} \mid \vm_t^\intercal\,\va_{t+1},\,
          \va_{t+1}^\intercal\,\vSigma_t\,\va_{t+1} + \sigma^2
     ),\\
\psi_t &\in \{0, 1, \ldots, t\} \\
p(\psi_t \mid \psi_{t-1}) &= \lambda \cdot \vone(\psi_t = 0) + (1-\lambda)\cdot \vone(\psi_t = \psi_{t-1}+1).
\end{aligned}
}
$$

<div v-click>
The predict step is
$$
\tag{M.3}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
     \vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t} &\gets {\rm predict}(\vb_{t-1}, \psi_t) = (\vm_{t-1}^{\psi_t}, \vSigma_{t-1}^{\psi_t}),\\
    \vm_{t-1}^{\psi_t} &= \vm_{t-1},\\
    \vSigma_{t-1}^{\psi_t} &= \vSigma_{t-1}\,\vone(\psi_t > 0) + \vSigma_0\,\vone(\psi_t = 0).
\end{aligned}
}
$$
</div>

<div v-click>
The update step is
$$
\tag{A.1}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
     \vb_t &\gets {\rm update}(\vb_{t-1}^{\psi_t}, \data_t) = (\vm_t, \vSigma_t),\\
     \vm_t &= \vm_{t-1}^{\psi_t} + \vK_t\,(y_t - (\vm_{t-1}^{\psi_t})^\intercal\,\va_t),\\
     \vSigma_t &= \vSigma_{t-1}^{\psi_t} - \vK_t\,\va_t^\intercal\,\vSigma_{t-1}^{\psi_t},\\
     \vK_t &= \frac{\vSigma_{t-1}^{\psi_t}\,\va_t}{\va_t^\intercal\,\vSigma_{t-1}^{\psi_t}\,\va_t + \sigma^2}.
\end{aligned}
}
$$
</div>

<div v-click>

The auxiliary update is the same as BOCD.
Evaluate for all $\psi_t$ and normalise:
$$
\tag{A.2}
\boxed{
     w_t(\psi_t) \propto
     \begin{cases}
     p_{\vb_t^{\psi_t}}(y_t \mid \vx_t)\, \sum_{\psi_{t-1}\in\Psi_{t-1}} p(\psi_t\mid\psi_{t-1})\,w_{t-1}(\psi_{t-1}),
     & \psi_t = 0,\\
     p_{\vb_t^{\psi_t}}(y_t \mid \vx_t)\, p(\psi_t\mid\psi_{t-1}=\psi_t-1)\,w_{t-1}(\psi_t-1),
     & \psi_t > 0.
     \end{cases}
}
$$
</div>

# Applications and current results


We instantiate BONE in four settings.
In each case, the same five choices (M.1, M.2, M.3, A.1, A.2) produce a domain-specific method:

1. Time series forecasting under non-stationarity
1. Online continual learning with neural networks
1. Bandits
1. Infinite hidden Markov models
1. Streaming reinforcement learning

---

## Application 1: Continual learning

Predictive-first perspective:
we track posterior predictive quality online under drift and changepoints.

One BONE instance:
* M.1: neural network measurement model $h(\theta, x)$.
* M.2: $RL$ or $CPP$.
* M.3: OUPR / OU / ACI conditional prior.
* A.1: linearised Kalman-style Gaussian update 
* A.2: posterior or point estimate over regime variable.

## Application 2: contextual bandits

Problem: non-stationary rewards, online action selection with exploration/exploitation.

One BONE instance:

* M.2: runlength ($RL$) or changepoint probability ($CPP$).
* M.3: prior reset ($PR$) or OU reversion.
* A.1: conjugate/Beta-Bernoulli or Gaussian approximation.
* A.2: Bayesian weights over regimes + Thompson sampling.

## Application 3: iHMM / switching state-space models

Problem: infer latent switching structure and predict observations under regime changes.

We assume piecewise-stationarity, so the environment is a switching state-space model (SSSM) with unknown number of regimes.
We need to balance the adapticity to creating a new outlier
and robustness to outliers that create spurious regimes.

* M.1: emission model for observations.
* M.2: discrete latent regime index (possibly countably infinite).
* M.3: regime-conditioned prior over latent state/parameters.
* A.1: variational or message-passing posterior update.
* A.2: posterior over regime assignments.

## iHMM: create regimes only when truly needed

In iHMM inference, the goal is selective adaptation:
create a new regime only when observations indicate a genuine regime change,
not when the data contain temporary noise or outliers.

## Application 4: streaming reinforcement learning

Problem: transition/reward dynamics shift over time while the agent is learning.

One BONE mapping:
* M.1: value/reward model (or dynamics model).
* M.2: latent non-stationarity variable over environment mode.
* M.3: conditional prior implementing reset/forgetting/adaptation.
* A.1: online posterior update for model parameters.
* A.2: weighting over environment modes.
